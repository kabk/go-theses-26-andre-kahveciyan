Abstract

This essay is about what it means to document a music scene from the inside—as a graphic designer, event organiser, and enjoyer active in Amsterdam's local underground music scene. For the past few years I have been building an archive—photographs taken before, during and after shows, setlists from stage floors, t-shirts from local bands, flyers. These aren't just documents. They're my journal.

The question driving this essay is how my position as organiser and observer shapes the way I see and document what's around me—and what that gaze produces that more conventional reporting doesn't. I'm never just a visitor at the events I organise, but I'm also not performing. I'm somewhere in between, and that position creates a specific kind of attention.

I approach this through my own archive and practice, in conversation with people and works that think about documentation and photography in similar ways. The essay is written in first person and accompanied by images from my archive.

This research feeds directly into my graduation work—a magazine that tries to document, celebrate and archive Amsterdam's underground music scene from this same position. Not from the outside looking in, but from someone who is already there.

Introduction

It's the 27th of April 2023, kingsnight. Dmitri, vocalist of INBRANDERS, and I end up at Café Minds where Teenage Tits are playing. The spot is tiny, the crowd moves together the whole set. Around midnight we get a tip from L to go to a squat in Amsterdam Noord, a warehouse around Papaverweg — there's a free party on KLOKI soundsystem. We stay until 10am. On the bike ride back we talk about wanting more from this scene than just going out and staying up till late. We want to do something for the bands that aren't getting booked, for the young people just starting to go out, for a culture that isn't being documented or represented properly. We make a promise to meet the next week and work it out and not let this energy go to waste.

That became KRUISPUNT. An event organisation we started together in Amsterdam, celebrating bands and movements around us that we felt needed more attention. A main goal from the start was to bring live music into club hours, where usually only DJs perform. Representation was important too—we wanted people of different backgrounds, ages and genders to feel like this was also for them. Since that night we've done an event almost every month in different venues across Amsterdam.

What I also remember from that night is that I had my camera with me. I wasn't thinking about documenting anything. One of the pictures I took at Minds that night ended up becoming the single cover for Rust, released by INBRANDERS in May 2023. We tried to do different things until I thought of this image.

That's the position I keep finding myself in. I'm not performing. I'm not a regular visitor either. I'm somewhere in between—part of the organisation of these nights, but also just moving through them with a camera, taking pictures of what's around me. In this essay I want to explore what that position actually is, how it shapes the way I look and document, and what it produces that a more conventional way of reporting doesn't.

The Third Role

When I'm at one of the events I organise I'm never just watching. But I'm also not performing. And I'm not a regular visitor either. I'm aware of what's happening around me in a different way—not because I'm looking for it but because I'm part of the organisation of the night. This is a position I kept finding myself in before I even had words for it.

It started when I was doing graphic design for Famirie, an event series run by Bureaupunt, a booking and management agency started by Ghamte Schmidt focused mostly on Black artists. I was making the posters and visuals but I was always at the events too. I wasn't just delivering a service. I was in the room, moving through it, watching. Design was my first way of belonging to a scene I cared about—it got me through the door in a role, and once I was inside I realised the watching was already happening naturally.

With KRUISPUNT that contribution became more concrete. But even within organising my own nights I'm still the one watching. I'm responsible for the night but I'm not on stage. I'm not dancing or losing myself in the music the way someone who just bought a ticket can. I'm somewhere in between all of it, conscious of the room in a specific way.

Nan Goldin, whose photo series The Ballad of Sexual Dependency worked as a visual diary of a scene she was completely inside, described her camera as a way of controlling what she was experiencing. I recognise something in that. The camera makes the watching feel intentional rather than just something that happens to me. It gives the position a function.

This is what I mean when I talk about a third role. Not performer, not visitor. Something in between that produces a specific kind of attention. And the pictures I take at events, on the way there, the day after—they're not just documentation. They're the closest thing I have to keeping a journal.

My Camera as a Journal

I've been taking pictures for a long time but I didn't always think of it as something serious. It was more like a reflex. I'd bring my Sony Cybershot to a show, to a party, to the bike ride there, and just point it at whatever was around me. Not always the artist on stage. Sometimes the people waiting outside, the street on the way, the moment before anything happened. Looking back at these images now I realise I was keeping track of my own life without really deciding to.

Chris Marker does something similar in Sans Soleil, his 1983 film essay built from footage and letters that reads more like a journal than a documentary. He collects images not to report on something but to hold onto it—to process time passing. What stays with me from that film is the idea that memory doesn't work in a straight line, it works in images that survive. That's close to what my archive feels like. Not a timeline but a collection of moments that proved something was happening.

Menno Otten's short film In Een Vergeten Moment (2009) gets at something related. Filmed at a tram stop in Amsterdam, it captures people in small in-between moments—waiting, not thinking about anything in particular, just existing. Otten was inspired by a photo by Ed van der Elsken of a woman in the Tokyo metro, completely lost in thought. What draws me to both of them is that the subject isn't the event, it's the space around it. That's where I also find myself pointing my camera. What also stays with me about the film is how it's built—the rhythm of it. Close faces, then blurry streaks of traffic, then faces again. It breathes.

Luc Delahaye's photography book L'Autre does something similar with its pacing—portraits interrupted by completely black pages, giving you space before the next face arrives. Shot on the Paris metro with a hidden camera, the images are of strangers who don't know they're being photographed. There's an intimacy in that—real expressions, unguarded faces. It raises questions about the ethics of looking that I think about in my own practice too. When I photograph at events I'm not hidden, but I'm also not always noticed. The people in my pictures are caught between performing and just being there, which is also where I am. I think about this rhythm when I think about how my magazine could work—these moments to pause, to not be overwhelmed, to just sit with one image before moving to the next.

Roland Barthes writes in Camera Lucida that a photograph doesn't just show what something looked like—it proves it existed. He calls this the "that-has-been." Every picture in my archive is that. Proof that I was in that room, that this band played, that this night happened.

What I chose to keep, let go, thought was worth holding onto. It reflects where I was standing, what I was paying attention to, and what felt important at that moment.

Collecting as Documenting

The pictures are one part of it. But there's also everything else I hold onto. Setlists picked up from the stage floor after a show, t-shirts from local bands, flyers, CDs. Physical proof that something happened, that a culture was alive at a specific moment. Not everything gets digitized. Not everything gets written about. A lot of it only survives because someone decided it was worth keeping. I think about people who go back and archive records and CDs from scenes that were never properly documented—like Rotational, who every week uploads music he finds on vinyl and tapes from his personal collection to YouTube, digitizing music from all over the world that would otherwise stay unheard.

I see the same impulse in the people around me. Yannesh Meijeman and Jonas Ganzeveld run Kromme Stad, an online film archive and curatorial platform focused on fiction and documentary films about urban power, resistance, graffiti and changing cityscapes—with a strong focus on Amsterdam. They show raw archival footage and organise screenings around spatial development and subcultures. What draws me to what they do is that it's not just preservation for its own sake—it's a way of showing what has been lost, what is still here, and who gets to decide what a city looks like.

Yannesh recently made a film called A Videographer Films Amsterdam, shown directly alongside Ed van der Elsken's 1982 film A Photographer Films Amsterdam—a camera on wheels moving through the city, documenting its state at that moment. Yannesh follows the same route, from Dam Square to the Albert Cuyp Market, and places his version next to Van der Elsken's. Half a century apart, the same streets, completely different cities. I remember him telling me about this idea when we went together to a screening of Hotel Mokum at Eye Filmmuseum, where they also showed parts of Van der Elsken's film. He told me he was really inspired and wanted to recreate it in his own way. It was nice to then follow his process and see it actually come to life—I saw the first version at my friend Layla Gijssen's show at W139, part of Spookstad. What stays with me about his project is something he said about it—that what he films isn't everybody's Amsterdam. It's his, right now. Someone else can do this again later, and it will be different again.

That's exactly what I feel about my own archive. It's not objective. It's not complete. It's one person's record of a specific moment in a specific scene, taken from a specific position. But that's also what makes it worth something.

In Rotation

There is no shortage of music journalism in the Netherlands. But if you follow the local underground scene closely, you start to notice that the same names keep coming back. Platforms like 3voor12 have found a way to report on a small rotation of bands — slightly under the mainstream radar but still connected enough to management or booking agencies that reach out on their behalf. The reporting follows the pitch, not the music. And you can feel it in the writing — there's nothing intimate about it, no real opinion, no sense that the person writing actually knows the room they're describing. For me, being associated with that kind of coverage has started to make an artist feel less interesting rather than more.

What's missing is a closeness. A sense that the person documenting is actually inside the thing they're writing about, not arriving from outside with a list of questions. I see this being filled in small ways by people around me. Jules van Eijs is 21 and has been following and photographing bands consistently for years—now one of the house photographers at Paradiso. Following his images gives you a better sense of what's actually happening in this scene than a 3voor12 article. It's not because he has more resources, it's because he's there everyday, and it shows in the pictures.

The same goes for Y.E.A.R.N, a platform started by Maria and Jeanne from Amsterdam band Prom Vagabonds. They do music reviews, concert vlogs, interviews, and republish music videos — all from inside the scene they love. Their bio describes it as wanting to connect audiences with new music and the behind the scenes of music life in local scenes. What makes it work is that it's real. You can feel that they care.

These are people doing what I want to do with my graduation work—a magazine where the conversations are with artists I'm already close to, images taken in vulnerable moments, a publication that feels like it comes from inside rather than looking in from outside. The position I described earlier—organiser, observer, photographer—is what makes that possible. I don't want to manufacture these conversations or images. More try to record what's already there.

Conclusion

This essay started with one night and one goal. But looking back at everything it describes—the events, the pictures, the things I collect, the people around me doing similar things—I realise none of it is really mine. The bands aren't mine, the venues aren't mine, the scene isn't mine. I just happen to be moving through it from a specific position, with a camera and an eye for what's happening around me.

And I'm also just someone who really loves this. I'm at every show, usually in front, not observing from a distance but fully in it. People know me, I know them. It's just that even while being in it I'm also trying to notice what's around me, to try to hold onto it somehow.

What I'm doing with my graduation work is to find a way to make that position visible. A magazine that works as an extension of this gaze—not me writing about myself but me pointing at everything around me that I think deserves more attention. The artists, the organisers, the photographers, the people just showing up every week to dance. The moments between the sets, the conversations after, the things that don't make it into any reporting.

I don't think my gaze is more important than anyone else's. But I do think it's specific. It comes from being inside this scene for a few years now, from organising, from designing, from photographing, from collecting. From being the person who is always there but never fully in one role. That position has given me something—a way of seeing that I'm still figuring out how to use. The magazine is where I want to try.
